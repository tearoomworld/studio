A weekly visit
between communities.

Kindred is the only software that lets nursing home residents spend an hour each week with residents at another community — playing Bingo, sharing stories, hearing music together. Not a screen replacement. A visiting day.

One product, three rhythms. Group visits weekly. Resident spotlights monthly. Pen pals ongoing. Activities directors run it. Residents experience it. Families notice it on tours.

What we ship · v1 to v2

Three rhythms
of connection.

Group visits, resident spotlights, and pen pals. Every resident gets the kind of connection that works at their pace.

v1 — the weekly visit

01
Bingo, the right way
Game
One caller, two rooms, one shared called number on screen. Both rooms cheer when someone wins. Universal, near-zero learning curve.
02
Decade Trivia
Game
1940s through 1970s. Questions about things 80-year-olds actually lived through. Auto-generated weekly, human-reviewed.
03
Name That Tune
Game
Music memory survives dementia longer than almost anything else. Era-appropriate clips. Both rooms sing the chorus together.
04
Memory Lane
Game
Storytelling prompts. "What was your first job?" "Describe Sunday dinners growing up." Least game-like, most connecting.

v1.5 — spotlights

Once a month, one resident is the expert.

Mr. Henderson on his 40 years working the railroad. Mrs. Patel's recipes from childhood in Mumbai. Eleanor on raising a family during Vietnam. A 20-minute talk, 10 minutes of questions, recorded with permission, sent to families as a keepsake.

The resident gets to be the expert again. That feeling is hard to manufacture and impossible to put a price on.

v2 — pen pals

The quiet residents need this most.

Group sessions are eight people once a week. Pen pals are one-to-one, ongoing, gentle. Letters, audio notes, short videos exchanged with a friend at another community. Directors read incoming letters aloud to residents who can't read easily anymore.

Slow, asynchronous, no bad days missed. It's the feature that turns Kindred from "video games for seniors" into a senior connection platform.

How the money works

Per facility, per month: $449.

Below the procurement-approval threshold at most facilities. Cheaper than the part-time activities assistant they'd consider hiring. About 0.05% of facility revenue — a rounding error to them, a real business for us.

Three tiers exist for chains: Starter at $249, Standard at $449, Network at $899 with volume discounts above five facilities.

Strategy · who, where, how

Activities directors
are the wedge.

Selling to nursing homes is selling to one person: the Activities Director. Win her, the facility follows. Win three in a region, the whole state's NAAP chapter knows your name within a month.

The core idea

Don't sell from the activities budget. Sell from the marketing budget.

Kindred isn't pitched as a tool that replaces in-person programming. It's pitched as a marketing differentiator. "Your residents have weekly visits with three sister communities. Tell that to the next family touring."

One additional resident attracted = $108,000/year in facility revenue. Eighteen days of one new resident covers Kindred for the entire year.

The brand does the work

Warm peach. Soft sage. Cream backgrounds. Fraunces italics. Generous whitespace. Kindred looks like a wellness brand, not a nursing home tech vendor. That difference matters. The Activities Director who clicks our link expects another sterile portal and finds something that looks like it was made with care. The pitch is half-done before the first word is read.

Launch — Atlanta first

Stay tight. Five facilities, all within thirty miles. Visit them weekly. Fix bugs in person. Build the playbook here, then repeat it in Birmingham, Charlotte, Nashville. The first city is a craft, not a campaign.

What stays the same

The product never gets more complicated than residents can handle.

Big buttons. Familiar games. Warm colors. One TV in the common room. Activities directors run it. That's true at five homes and it's true at five thousand.

Every new feature has to pass the grandma test. If she can't use it, it doesn't ship.
