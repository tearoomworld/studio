the build plan

eight weeks from prototype to a native app live in the app store and google play. the work splits into four build weeks, one to go native, and a submission window paced by apple's review queue.

the shape

build
4 wks
app, database, tickets, hosts
go native
1 wk
wrap, icons, store prep
submit
2–3 wks
apple review loop

why eight, not four. the hands-on build is ~4 weeks. the rest is apple's bureaucracy — llc, d-u-n-s number, developer enrollment, app review — which runs in the background while the build continues. the one thing outside our control is apple's queue.

week one — foundation

tools installed, the app running locally, and the slow paperwork started so it processes for weeks while we build.

days 1–5dev environment, accounts, the app on-screen
node, xcode, vs code, github. supabase + vercel + google play accounts. the prototype foundation runs locally on real database data. llc filing and the d-u-n-s number start now as background tasks.

week two — real content

the app stops being a demo: real atlanta events, real photos, real copy.

days 6–1015 real events, photo uploads, apple enrollment
the seed becomes 15 real may–july atlanta events. photo upload works end to end. apple developer enrollment begins once the llc + d-u-n-s clear.

week three — accounts & tickets

people can sign in and buy a ticket. money is involved, so everything is tested twice.

days 11–15sign-in, stripe, ticketing, confirmation emails
the lightweight "buy door" — a one-screen sign-in at checkout. stripe wired, fee math confirmed (10% + $0.99 per ticket), test purchases, then real payments live.

week four — hosts, the pool & the admin dashboard

hosts can sign up, get paid, and post. the pool runs. and we get a private place to operate the business.

days 16–19host onboarding, stripe connect, posting, the pool
the guided "host door": profile, payout setup, posting events. stripe connect pays hosts automatically. the pool tally and host leaderboard go live.
day 20backend admin dashboardnew
a private, founders-only dashboard: every event and host at a glance, ticket sales and revenue, the live pool tally, and a "mark winner" action to pay out the quarterly prize. lightweight by design — supabase already gives a raw table view, so this is just the summaries and actions that need to be one click.

week five — becoming a real app

the finished web app gets wrapped into real iphone + android apps.

days 21–25capacitor wrap, app icon, store listings, testflight
capacitor turns one codebase into native apps — no rewrite. app icon, store screenshots, privacy disclosures, and a testflight beta to real phones, including the launch hosts'.

weeks six–eight — submit & launch

submit to both stores, work the review loop, launch together.

days 26+submission, review rounds, public launch
submit to apple + google. expect 2–3 review rounds — normal for a first app. when both approve, source launches in both stores with the three confirmed hosts posting the first real events.

how the money works

source takes a fee on every paid ticket: 10% of the ticket price, plus $0.99 per ticket. a quarter of that fee funds the pool — the quarterly prize for a top-rated host. the rest is source's revenue. free events carry no ticket fee.

the fee is deliberately small — hosts keep the overwhelming majority of ticket revenue. the pitch to a host: keep your audience, keep your money, and a stake in a quarterly prize.

the flywheel

01tickets fund the pool. a slice of every paid ticket builds a quarterly prize.
02the prize attracts hosts. top-rated hosts compete for it — a reason to host on source, not instagram.
03more hosts, more events. each host brings their own audience to the feed.
04more events, a bigger pool. which makes the prize more compelling — and the loop tightens.

beyond year one

year-one revenue from a single city is modest by design — the low fee is the cost of winning hosts and proving the loop. the thesis is the per-city multiplier: the model is built to repeat in la, new york, and london, each with its own pool and its own scene. the database is already hub-aware — a second city is a switch, not a rebuild. five cities at the strong-case run-rate is a materially different business.

marketing strategy

source doesn't win by out-spending anyone. it wins by being the place atlanta's scene already trusts — built host-first, told in public, in a voice that never sounds like marketing.

the core idea

events already happen — they're just scattered across instagram, tiktok, and group chats, found by accident or not at all. source isn't creating demand; it's consolidating attention that already exists. the marketing job isn't persuasion. it's being where the scene already is, and being obviously useful.

hosts are the wedge

the whole strategy in one line: win the host, and their audience comes with them. a venue or collective that posts on source brings their existing followers to the feed. one good host is worth more than any ad — it's audience acquisition disguised as supply acquisition.

and this is source's structural advantage: host acquisition is largely solved before launch. the founder has direct reach into 15–20 hosts in the first months — the cold-start problem that kills most marketplaces is, here, a network the founder already has.

01start with the founder's network. the first wave of hosts comes from existing relationships in the atlanta scene — recruited directly, by hand.
02let hosts recruit hosts. scenes are small and connected. a host who keeps their money and likes the tool tells the next one.
03the pool is the closer. "a quarterly prize, funded by tickets, for top-rated hosts" — a reason to choose source no other platform offers.

building in public — the signature play

source's launch story isn't an ad campaign — it's the build itself. document making the app, step by step, and release it as content: the prototype, the design choices, the pool idea, the first hosts, the first ticket sold. take people through the process as it happens.

this does three things at once. it builds an audience before launch — people follow a story, not a product. it earns trust with hosts, who see a real founder doing real, careful work, not a faceless platform. and it's authentically on-brand — source is editorial, transparent, anti-hype; a build-in-public series is that voice, extended. it costs nothing but honesty.

01the making-of series. short, regular posts — designing the feed, the lowercase rule, wiring the pool, the first host conversations.
02show the why. not just "we built a screen" — "here's why a slice of every ticket goes back to a host." the mission, in public.
03launch to a warm audience. by the time the app is live, the people who watched it get built are the first to download — and the first to tell others.

instagram & tiktok — the home channels

this is where atlanta's scene already lives, so this is where source lives too. not as an advertiser — as a presence worth following in its own right.

01the build-in-public series
the making-of content, posted as it happens. tiktok for reach and discovery, instagram for the editorial cut. the founder's story is the hook.
02the feed, restyled for social
what's on this week, in source's editorial style — lowercase, photo-led, calm. a reason to follow even before downloading. the app's aesthetic, extended.
03host spotlights
short features on the hosts and the nights they run. promotes hosts, deepens the partnership, and gives them content to reshare to their own audiences.
04the pool announcements
each quarterly award is a built-in content moment — a named winner, a real amount, a story about money moving back to culture. four a year, by design.

the brand does the work

source looks like a gallery catalog, not a tech app — lowercase, editorial, calm, photo-led. in a feed of loud, templated event promos, that restraint is the differentiator. the brand signals taste, and taste is what the atlanta arts scene responds to. the rule for every post, everywhere: never sound like marketing. plain, lowercase, in voice — the same register as the app itself.

launch — atlanta only, on purpose

source launches in one city and says so plainly. that focus is a strength: a credible, dense presence in one scene beats a thin presence across five. the beta's goal isn't scale — it's proof: that hosts stay, that the pool funds, that the loop turns. proof is what makes the second city, and any raise, real.

the one-line strategy. win atlanta's hosts through the founder's network, build the app in public so launch meets a warm audience, let the pool give everyone a story worth repeating — and never sound like an ad. spend on nothing until the loop is proven, then repeat it city by city.

the five-year vision

where source goes if the loop holds — from one feed in atlanta to cultural infrastructure across a network of cities, each with its own scene, its own pool, its own people getting paid.

vision — a picture of the ambition, not a forecast

the picture

five years out, source isn't an events app. it's the layer a city's culture runs on. in every city it's in, the question "what's on this week" has one answer — and the people who throw those nights keep their audiences, keep their money, and have a real stake in a prize that pays out every quarter.

the model proven in atlanta — host-first, pool-funded, editorial — repeats city by city. not franchised, not templated: each city onboarded the way that city actually works, the way the brief always said. the pool becomes a quiet, recurring act of redistribution at city scale — culture funding culture, on a loop.

the pool cap — how it scales

a quarterly pool that grew without limit would eventually distort everything — a runaway prize warps incentives. so the pool is capped at $10,000 per city, per quarter. it stays a serious, life-changing prize for a host — but a sane one.

once a city's quarterly pool hits the cap, the overflow flows to source. this is the mechanic that makes mission and business agree: young cities pay everything to hosts (no city hits the cap early — exactly when hosts matter most), and mature cities generate overflow that funds the company. the system self-adjusts.

one mature city, per quarteramount
raw pool share (25% of fees)~$19,500
— pool, capped$10,000
— overflow to source~$9,500

the host prize stays meaningful and stable; source captures the upside of a city's maturity. the cap is a feature, not a limit.
